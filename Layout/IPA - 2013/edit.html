Integrated Polymer-to-Metal Interface Sealing Process for Exhaust Component Applications
Business
SBU
Pattern Type
Business Unit
Segment
Application
Product
Project
NOI Approval Manager
Problem
Rich text editor Problem

Most internal combustion engines utilize some form of exhaust muffler to reduce exhaust sound levels. When manufacturing exhaust mufflers consisting of polymeric casings assembled over metal internal components, the interface between the polymeric casing and the metal piece(s) must provide an adequate seal to prevent leakage of exhaust gasses from the muffler assembly. This presents challenges due to the tolerance stack-up of the related components, as well as the dissimilar coefficients of thermal expansion. Abnormal levels of leakage (defined by end-user requirements) can result in unwanted NVH (Noise, Vibration, and Harshness) characteristics, potentially hazardous CO (carbon monoxide) emissions, and the inability of the muffler unit to contain and channel hot exhaust gasses throughout the rest of the exhaust system.

Approaches
Rich text editor Approaches

For similar applications, some form of seal was required to be placed at the interface between the polymeric casing and mating metal components. Sealing methods included, but were not limited to O-rings (made from high-temperature silicone or Vamac (R)) , press-fit bushings made from a thermoset polymer, high temperature thermoplastic polymer such as Teflon(R), an infusible polymer, such as Vespel(R), or some form of liquid-applied high-temperature sealant, such as RTV (Room Temperature Vulcanizing) silicone, or the like. All of these methods increase the overall system cost through the addition of pieces and assembly steps. These methods also limit the design flexibility of the polymeric casing due to the requirement of a continuously-molded orifice to form the composite/metal interface. Some examples using O-ring seals for muffler applications are outlined here: [Si Seal Props_083109.pdf]


Others Solution
Rich text editor Others Solution

The polymeric pieces to be assembled are welded together via the linear vibration welding method.  In this method, one piece is held stationary while the other mating piece is vibrated against it in order to generate sufficient frictional heating in order to melt the components into one another. This invention makes additional use of this frictional heating to melt-form a plurality of V-shaped ribs molded into the polymeric casing along the perimeter of the composite/metal interface. The number of ribs required is determined through the use of Finite Element Analysis (FEA). The ribs on the stationary polymeric part are sized to the nominal dimension of the mating metal piece, while the ribs on the moving polymeric part are designed with an interference, the exact size of which is determined though component assembly testing and tolerance studies. During the welding process, the polymeric ribs are compressed against the mating metal component and vibrated against it. The friction between the ribs and metal generates sufficient heat to melt the tips of the ribs on the moving polymeric part, allowing them to form to the contours of the metal piece. The pressure exerted on the plastic pieces causes the ribs on the stationary polymeric part to deform slightly in order to match the contours of the metal piece. Once the polymeric pieces have welded together, the vibration stops and the parts are held together under pressure to permit the molten polymer to re-solidify. This way, despite the tolerances of both the metal and polymeric components, a "custom fit" can be achieved at the interface between the two. This also eliminates the need for any type of mechanical seal, thus reducing cost and assembly steps.
Utility
Rich text editor Utility

The invention would be used to lock in DuPont Engineering Polymers products for use in exhaust system components for applications ranging from Automotive to Industrial/Consumer applications.  
Competitive
Rich text editor Competitive

Reference patents/IP include AD7604, AD7609 (both recently combined into one case), and other polymer composite muffler related patents.
Earliest Document
Rich text editor Earliest Document

The date of conception for this invention was September 1st, 2009 during a brainstorming meeting between DuPont, Roush Industries and Branson Corp. During the following week aspects of feasibility, design and analysis were carried out. Attached is a dated sketch outlining the basic concept: [CM Vib Rib Proposal_Confidential_20090902.pdf]

A proof-of-concept prototype was tested on September 25th, 2009 at the Branson Technical Center in Michigan. It consisted of a metal tube vibrated against a polymeric material plaque that had the intended rib shape machined into it. A CAD model screen shot of the plaque and trial rib shape is attached: [propA_id93root_w15.5_wa2_4land_block_iso.jpg]

I have e-mails, meeting notices, etc. that also document these dates.

Disclosure
Other
Rich text editor Other

As mentioned above, the invention was described during a meeting between DuPont, Roush and Branson on September 1st, 2009. Discussed this idea with the other attendees at this meeting: Michael Deneau, Roush Program Management; Daniel Dureno, Roush Project Engineering Supervisor; and Colin Drewek, Authorized Field Representative for Branson Corp. The purpose of this meeting was to come up with ways we could weld muffler casing halves together in a manner that would permit a good seal between the casing and the metal pipe running through it. This idea proved to be the most practical and viable option.
